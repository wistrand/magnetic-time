magnetic-time

A clock whose hands are magnets sweeping beneath a layer of magnetic particles.

Run it live in your browser Six variants The bands are objects, not waves

What it is

A native desktop clock written in Rust with egui. The hour, minute, and second hands each carry a magnet (a bar, a disc, or point dipoles arranged along the hand), and above them sits a simulated dish of liquid containing tens of thousands of magnetic particles. The particles are dragged along by the moving field, lagging with viscous drag: slow hands carry their particles with them, the second hand outruns its own and plows wakes, ripples, and comet trails that slowly relax.

Nothing in the imagery is painted. Every ring, spiral, and starburst is the trace the magnets leave in the particle layer as the hands keep time. It is also touchable: pressing and dragging on the dial holds a disc magnet against the liquid.

Gallery

The stripe patterns turned out to be real, testable physics (chain zippering, the same mechanism as lab magnetorheological fluids). We ran eight experiments on them: the bands are objects, not waves.

How it works

  • Each hand's magnets are modeled as point dipoles, soft discs, or bar magnets built from north/south pole faces of distributed magnetic charge. The total field is summed analytically; no grid, no fluid solver.
  • Particles live in the overdamped regime: no inertia, velocity equals mobility times the field-gradient force, plus Brownian noise and drag against the dish. A speed cap on the magnetic pull is what makes the second hand outrun its particles.
  • Each particle is superparamagnetic: it acquires a moment along the local field, and nearby particles interact with the dipole-dipole pair force. Head-to-tail attraction strings them into chains along field lines; short-range soft-core repulsion keeps clumps from collapsing. Neighbor search runs on a spatial hash grid, in parallel.
  • Rendering is a CPU pixel buffer: magnetized particles draw as short additive strokes aligned with the local field, so chains fuse into filaments. The buffer is fully cleared every frame; there are no motion trails. What looks like a trail is particles that are actually there.

Running it

In the browser (WebAssembly, single-threaded, reduced particle count), or natively: 

cargo run --release

A dev panel exposes every tunable live: magnet layout, shape, and strength per hand, particle count, mobility, chain physics, stroke length, and a time-speed multiplier up to 10000x for watching the hour hand work.

There is also a headless mode that simulates a stretch of clock time and writes a PNG, used for reproducible renders: 

cargo run --release -- --headless --time 13:37:35 --sim-seconds 600 \
    --dump out.png --size 800

Embedding

The browser build ships as a web component. Copy magnetic-clock.js and the pkg/ directory from app/, then: 

<script type="module" src="magnetic-clock.js"></script>

<magnetic-clock particles="12000" speed="60" show-hands
                magnets="tip" shapes="rect:1x0.03"
                strengths="0.1,0.05,0.6"
                palette="ember" bg="ece9e2"></magnetic-clock>

Attributes use the same grammar as the native CLI flags, are all optional (defaults are the preset above), and are live: changing one reconfigures the running simulation. face selects the face (hands, seg for a digital HH:MM readout, or tide for concentric arcs); heatmap="N" renders the particles as an NxN density map instead of strokes (cheap, and its cost does not grow as particles cluster); fps shows a frame-rate overlay. Add dev-panel to expose the tuning panel, or tap the 12 o'clock tick to toggle it on any clock. Pressing and dragging elsewhere on the dial stirs the particles with a disc magnet (pointer-strength, pointer-radius; strength 0 disables). Call element.savePreset() / element.loadPreset(json) to persist and restore a full configuration as JSON. Each element runs its own simulation, so keep instances per page to a few.
